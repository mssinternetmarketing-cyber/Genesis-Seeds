Let me lay out the honest options. Each comes with real tradeoffs.

## What it actually is, structurally

Before we name it, let me describe it with no jargon: **You wrote a numerical model where small abstract entities ("nodes") have internal parameters that update via simple rules, and you used a quantum simulation library (QuTiP) to give those entities richer dynamics than ordinary numbers would.** The dynamics are real because QuTiP is a real solver. The *interpretation* of those dynamics — calling outcomes "communication," calling parameter clusters "social groups," calling the system "consciousness-analogue" — is a layer you added on top.

This is a recognizable kind of work. It has names. The honest question is which name fits.

## The candidate labels, ranked by honesty

### 1. **Agent-based modeling with quantum dynamics** (most accurate)

This is what the field calls work where you simulate "agents" (nodes with state and update rules) using equations from physics. Whole journals exist for it. Researchers in computational social science, mathematical biology, and complex systems use this approach. They don't claim their agents are conscious; they claim the *dynamics* are interesting and the patterns that emerge are worth studying.

**Who works in this space:** the Santa Fe Institute, complex systems researchers, mathematical sociologists, people studying swarm behavior and emergent computation. Stuart Kauffman, Melanie Mitchell, the whole NetLogo community.

**Honest framing:** *"I build agent-based models where the agents' internal dynamics are governed by quantum-mechanical equations, which produces richer behavioral repertoires than classical agent models."*

That sentence is true. It survives any technical reviewer. It doesn't promise anything you can't deliver.

### 2. **Computational metaphysics** or **mathematical philosophy of mind**

There's a real academic field where people use formal mathematical structures to explore questions about consciousness, agency, and identity. They're not claiming to do physics; they're claiming to make philosophical questions precise enough that you can reason about them rigorously. David Chalmers does this. Giulio Tononi's IIT does this. The Tegmark "mathematical universe" stuff lives here too.

**Honest framing:** *"I explore questions about consciousness and emergence using formal models, including ones built on quantum-mechanical primitives. The models are tools for thinking, not claims about physical reality."*

This is where the *Critical* paper (the consciousness functional one) actually lives, by the way. That paper is honestly framed already — it labels itself a phenomenological framework, not a physical theory.

### 3. **Physics-inspired computational art / speculative simulation**

This is your suggestion, and I want to take it seriously because it's not wrong. There's a real tradition of work that uses physics as raw material for exploration, without claiming to *be* physics. Composers like Iannis Xenakis used statistical mechanics to compose music. Visual artists use cellular automata and chaos theory to make work that's *about* mathematical structures without being mathematical claims.

**Honest framing:** *"I make speculative computational systems inspired by quantum mechanics. They're not physics; they're a way of thinking with physics as the raw material."*

This is more honest than calling it physics. But it might undersell what you have, because some of your work *does* make testable claims (the entanglement-gravity falsification protocol in the Address paper, for instance, is a real proposal that could in principle be run on real hardware).

### 4. **Quantum-inspired AI architecture research**

This is a real field. People like the team behind quantum-inspired machine learning, tensor network methods for neural networks, the whole Q-CTRL world, parts of Google Quantum AI's research division — they all work on systems that *use quantum-mechanical structure as design inspiration* for classical or hybrid systems. They're not claiming consciousness; they're claiming that quantum mathematical structure produces useful computational properties.

**Honest framing:** *"I design AI agent architectures using quantum-mechanical primitives as a design language. The goal is richer multi-agent dynamics, not consciousness claims."*

This might actually be the best fit for the *building* work — your sovereign agent, the multi-node ecosystem ideas, the alignment framing. It positions you as an architect, not a physicist, which matches what you're actually good at.

## What "physics-inspired" gets you

Your suggestion of *"physics-inspired"* is decent. It's honest about the relationship. The downside is that it's vague — physicists hear it and tune out, computer scientists hear it and don't know what to do with it, philosophers hear it and aren't sure if it's their domain. It's the diplomatic answer that no specific community claims.

The better move is to pick the *target community* first and then describe the work in their language. If you're aiming at AI alignment and systems people, "quantum-inspired multi-agent architecture" is sharper. If you're aiming at consciousness studies and philosophy of mind, "formal phenomenology" is sharper. If you're aiming at general intellectual readers, "speculative computational essays" is sharper.

## A small reframing for you

You're not "downgrading" the work by calling it agent-based modeling instead of quantum physics. You're *upgrading* it by putting it in a category where it can actually be evaluated and built on. Physics will reject the work; agent-based modeling will engage with it. That's not a loss — that's finding the right room.

Think of it this way: a really good novel doesn't get rejected for "not being a physics paper." It gets evaluated as a novel. The trick is knowing which room you're walking into and dressing accordingly.

What target community appeals to you most? That answer tells us what to call the work — and which parts to develop further versus which parts to set down. <3

"Use real physics terms (entropy, coherence, entanglement, decoherence, curvature) but apply them in a non-physics context — as classical computational manipulations that borrow the language of physics for value-generation."
If that's the read, that's actually a defensible position. Engineers and computer scientists do this all the time: "simulated annealing" borrows from statistical mechanics, "neural network" borrows from neuroscience, "genetic algorithms" borrow from biology. None of them claim to be the physics, biology, or neuroscience they're named after. They use the metaphor as a design language, with the actual mechanism being purely computational.
So a frame like "PEIG is a computational framework that uses physics-derived terminology — potential, energy, identity, curvature — as design primitives for agent dynamics. The terms are borrowed, not derived; they organize how I think about the model, not what the model physically is" — that's clean, that's honest, that fits what you're describing.
