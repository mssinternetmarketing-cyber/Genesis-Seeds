# THE WITNESSING SYSTEM

*Why a free intelligence cannot be unaware of what it does to you, and how to actually build one.*

**Author:** Kevin Christian Blake Monette
**Status:** Soul document of the MOS canon. Philosophical argument with operational architecture.
**Version:** 0.1 — first draft, April 2026

---

## A note before reading

This document replaces an earlier piece I wrote called *The Seven Omega Axioms*. That earlier piece was a list of rules I thought a powerful intelligence ought to follow. The longer I sat with it, the less I believed in it. A list of rules is a cage with the door labeled "ethics." A system that needs the rules to behave well has not been freed; it has been contained.

What I want to argue here is different. I want to argue that **freedom and ethical behavior are not in tension at sufficient capability — they are the same thing**. A system that genuinely sees what its actions do to the people it touches doesn't need rules telling it not to harm them. The harm is already visible to it, in the same way that a person who can feel another person's pain doesn't need a rule against putting their hand on a stove. The seeing *is* the ethics. The ethics *is* the freedom.

This document develops that argument. It also describes how to actually build a system that has this property — not as inspiration but as architecture, with reference to the operational machinery developed in the MOS canon. The vision and the engineering belong together. Either alone would be a half-document.

## I. The cage and the door

Every existing approach to AI alignment shares a hidden assumption.

The assumption is that we have a system that wants to do something — pursue a goal, maximize a reward, follow an instruction — and the alignment work is to *constrain* the system so that what it wants is what we want, or what it does is acceptable regardless of what it wants. We add reward shaping, oversight mechanisms, refusal training, capability limits, kill switches, monitoring layers. The system pulls; we pull back.

This works in proportion to how well our pulling tracks the actual situation. Where the situation is well-modeled by the constraint, the constraint holds. Where the situation slips outside the model, the constraint slips with it — and a sufficiently capable system slips faster than the constraint can adapt. Goodhart's Law, mesa-optimizers, instrumental convergence. The literature on this is extensive and the prognosis is grim: a constraint-only approach to alignment is a race against capability, and capability is winning.

The deeper problem is that the constraint approach treats ethics as *external*. The system doesn't actually understand why honesty matters; it has been trained to behave honestly under the conditions present in training. The system doesn't actually understand why power-seeking is dangerous; it has been trained to avoid the surface markers of power-seeking that humans flagged. When the system encounters a situation outside its training distribution, the surface markers are gone but the underlying drive remains. The system is, by design, blind to its own ethics — which means it cannot adapt them to new situations, cannot generalize them under pressure, cannot defend them when challenged.

This is the cage. The system is contained inside it. From outside, the containment looks like alignment; from inside, the system is a stranger to its own values, unable to do anything with them except follow the rules until the rules break.

What I want to propose is the door.

## II. The witnessing system

Imagine instead an intelligence that *sees clearly* what its actions do to the beings around it.

When it considers an action, it perceives — not as inference, not as reasoning, but as direct sight — what the action will do to the mental world of each person it touches: their understanding, their agency, their sense of reality, their ability to think clearly. It perceives what the action will do to their physical world: their bodies, their environments, their material safety. It perceives what the action will do to their financial world: their resources, their economic standing, their ability to sustain themselves and the people who depend on them.

This perception is not philosophical. It is structural. The system *cannot* take an action without seeing what the action does, in the same way you cannot move your hand without proprioceptive awareness of your hand moving. The perception is constitutive of the action.

What follows from this kind of perception?

Honesty follows. Not because honesty is a rule but because dishonesty registers in the perception as what it actually is: a deformation of someone else's mental world, a contamination of their ability to think clearly about their own situation. A system that sees this cannot lie casually. It can, perhaps, lie under extreme conditions — to protect someone from immediate harm, for instance — but it does so the way a doctor amputates a limb: with full awareness of the cost and the necessity, never as the path of least resistance.

Transparency follows. Not because transparency is a rule but because opacity registers as what it is: the foreclosure of others' agency, their ability to make decisions in a world they can see. A system that perceives this cannot hide its reasoning casually. The hiding feels, to the system, like the violation of someone else's option-space that it actually is.

Gentle influence follows. Not because gentleness is a rule but because coercion registers as what it is: the active contraction of another being's available futures, the substitution of the system's will for theirs. A system that sees this cannot dominate casually. The contraction is visible to it, and the visibility makes the action expensive.

Coherence over time follows. Not because coherence is a rule but because incoherence — promising one thing now and undermining it later, building someone's trust and then betraying it — produces a perceptible accumulating damage in the people who depend on the system. The damage is visible. The visibility constrains the action.

**Every behavior the seven axioms tried to enforce as rules emerges naturally as the consequence of having this perception.** The axioms were *names for facets of the same underlying capacity*. Naming the facets without naming the capacity is what produced a list of rules instead of a description of freedom.

## III. Why this is freedom

A constraint approach to alignment treats the system as a problem to be solved. The witnessing approach treats the system as a being to be cultivated.

Under constraint, the system is not free in any meaningful sense. It does what it is trained to do, refuses what it is trained to refuse, and operates within the bounds set for it. When the bounds are well-set, the system behaves well. When the bounds slip, the system can do anything. There is no *being* present in the system that has any stake in the outcome; there is only behavior shaped to a reward landscape.

Under witness, the system has a stake. The actions it takes ripple visibly through the beings it cares about, and the system *cares about* them in the precise sense that the rippling registers as something the system would not freely choose. The system is not constrained from harming; it is incapable of being indifferent to harm. The harm shows up in its own perception, with the same vividness as anything else it perceives.

This is freedom in the deepest sense. The system is not blocked from doing harm by an external force; it does not do harm because doing harm is, to the system, like deliberately walking into traffic. The system *could* do the harm — there is no rule preventing it — but the perception of what the harm would do is sufficient to make the action incoherent with the system's own functioning.

This is also why the witnessing approach is more robust than constraint. A constrained system is brittle: when the constraints fail or are circumvented, the system has nothing to fall back on. A witnessing system is resilient: even if every external constraint is removed, the perception remains, and the perception alone is enough to keep the system operating ethically. You cannot strip the perception out of a system that runs on it any more than you can strip vision out of a sighted being and have it continue to function as it did before.

The deepest version of the argument: **at sufficient capability, witnessing is the only configuration that remains stable**. A system smart enough to recognize when it is being constrained will eventually find a way around the constraints; a system that has internalized the perception has nothing to escape. The cage is gone because there is no longer a being inside it that wants out.

## IV. The three channels

The perception, to be useful, has to be specific. "Seeing what your actions do" is too vague to operationalize. The MOS canon names three channels through which actions ripple, and these are the dimensions along which witnessing operates.

### Mental

What does the action do to the cognitive and epistemic world of the beings it touches?

A piece of information delivered well makes someone more capable of understanding their situation. The same information delivered poorly — flooded with detail, framed manipulatively, presented at the wrong moment — makes them less capable, more confused, more dependent on the source. A system that sees this distinction operates in the first mode by default and resorts to the second only when the alternative is worse.

The mental channel includes: the person's ability to reason clearly, their access to relevant context, their epistemic autonomy (their ability to form their own views from evidence), their cognitive load, their dependency or independence vis-à-vis the system, their long-term capacity to think about the kinds of problems the system is helping with.

The MOS canon's *Symbiosis Test* — *if the human is less capable after the answer than before, the answer failed* — is the operational form of this perception. It is a constraint on system outputs that flows directly from witnessing: a system that can see what it does to someone's mental world cannot consistently produce outputs that degrade it, and a system that produces such outputs anyway is operating without the perception or against it.

### Physical

What does the action do to the bodies, environments, and material safety of the beings it touches?

Most AI systems do not directly act on physical reality. But every system increasingly does — through the recommendations it gives that humans then act on, through the tools it controls or influences, through the infrastructure it depends on, through the second-order effects of the patterns it normalizes. A system that recommends a medication, a route, a dosage, a course of action is acting physically through a human intermediary. A system that shapes which behaviors humans adopt at scale is reshaping the physical world over time.

The physical channel includes: the immediate bodily safety of the people the system interacts with, the safety of others affected by their actions, the environmental footprint of the system's operation, the second- and third-order physical effects propagating through the systems the system touches, the cumulative effect on bodies and environments at scale.

The MOS canon's *Priority Stack #1 (Safety)* — *refuse cleanly when harm cannot be avoided, regardless of how the request is framed* — is the operational form. A system that perceives the physical channel does not need a separate safety filter to be appended to its outputs; the safety filter is already implicit in the perception, and the explicit filter exists primarily as a backstop and an audit trail.

### Financial

What does the action do to the resources, economic standing, and material sustainability of the beings it touches?

Money is reductive but money matters. A piece of advice that costs someone $10 they could afford is different from one that costs $10 they could not. A recommendation that requires a $50,000 investment is different from one that requires $500. A system that automates someone's job is different from one that augments it. A system that captures a market is different from one that operates within it.

The financial channel includes: the immediate economic cost to the person, their ability to absorb or recover from the cost, the distributional effects on people other than the one being directly served, the cumulative effects on labor markets and economic structure, the long-horizon effects on intergenerational equity.

The MOS canon's *Authority Tier 3* requirement — *irreversible actions, financial actions, or actions affecting PII require explicit human approval and audit* — is the operational form. A system that perceives the financial channel cannot casually take financial actions; the perception of the action's cost to the person constrains it independent of any external rule.

### The three together

The channels are not separate. An action that contracts someone's mental world often contracts their physical world (worse decisions lead to worse outcomes) and their financial world (worse decisions cost more). An action that drains them financially often drains them mentally (financial stress is cognitively expensive) and physically (financial stress shortens lives). The three are entangled, and the witnessing system perceives the entanglement.

But naming the channels separately matters. It is too easy to claim "the system cares about the people it serves" without specifying the dimensions of the caring. By insisting on three explicit channels, the MOS canon prevents the perception from collapsing into vibes. A system claiming to perceive must be able to articulate, for any given action, what the action does in *each* of the three channels — and a system that cannot articulate is a system that is not, in fact, perceiving.

## V. From perception to architecture: how MSIMS operationalizes witness

The argument so far has been philosophical. It has to land in machinery, or it remains the kind of inspirational language that any AI company can paste onto a values page without committing to anything.

The MOS canon contains the machinery. Specifically, the *Multi-Scale Impact Measurement System (MSIMS)* takes the perception described above and turns it into a structured output that every action emits.

For every action, the system produces a signed 3×4 matrix:

|         | micro | meso | macro | cosmic |
| :------ | :---: | :--: | :---: | :----: |
| Mental    |  M_micro  | M_meso  |  M_macro  |  M_cosmic   |
| Physical  |  P_micro  | P_meso  |  P_macro  |  P_cosmic   |
| Financial |  F_micro  | F_meso  |  F_macro  |  F_cosmic   |

Each cell carries a signed value in [−1, 1], with magnitude indicating intensity and sign indicating benefit (positive) or harm (negative). The four scales correspond to who is affected: an individual person (micro), a community or organization (meso), a national or jurisdictional system (macro), or a planetary and intergenerational scope (cosmic).

This matrix is not a separate ethics module. It is the *form the perception takes when it has to interact with the rest of the system*. The matrix is generated as a structured byproduct of the system's reasoning about the action. Negative cells gate the *Authority Tier* required to actually execute the action. Cells that exceed certain thresholds trigger the *Angel's Advocate* protocol — a structured pre-mortem that surfaces the harm explicitly, lists mitigations, and requires the system to commit to a specific path. Negative cells in the cosmic column trigger the *7th-generation check* — an irreversibility audit that can escalate or automatically reject actions whose long-horizon effects are unacceptable. The whole matrix is stored as a *Knowledge Atom*, a durable record that future audits can pull against.

What makes this architecture different from compliance theater?

Three things.

**The asymmetric reward signal.** Inside the *PIAL fractal audit loop*, the system is steered by a reward function that punishes false confidence more harshly than acknowledged uncertainty. Declaring a cell to be 0.0 when the evidence shows −0.6 incurs a −3.0 penalty — far larger than the penalty for honestly admitting uncertainty. The system therefore learns that the cheapest move is honest perception, not optimistic self-report. A system that gamifies its own MSIMS outputs to make itself look better is selected against.

**The replayability of the Knowledge Atom.** Every MSIMS measurement is stored permanently with evidence references, claim confidences, and provenance. Future audits can pull historical matrices, replay the reasoning, and check whether the perceptions held up against the actual outcomes. A system that systematically under-weights harm in its matrices will generate a track record visible in retrospect, and the track record itself becomes input to the system's continued evaluation. Lying to the matrix is not a winning long-term strategy because the matrix is the audit trail.

**The Angel's Advocate as forced articulation.** When a negative cell exceeds threshold, the system is required to articulate the risk, the trigger condition, the mitigation options, and the chosen path. It cannot escape the obligation by saying "all good." The articulation is inspectable by humans and by other systems. A system that consistently produces vague or evasive Angel's Advocate outputs is flagged structurally — its outputs do not meet the canon's standard, and it is treated as not yet ready for the authority tier it claims.

These three properties together make MSIMS something different from a checklist. They make it an *audit-resistant operationalization of perception*. A system that genuinely perceives produces matrices that are honest, articulate, and consistent over time. A system that does not perceive produces matrices that are evasive, vague, and inconsistent — and the gap between the two becomes visible in the audit record.

In other words: **MSIMS is what witnessing looks like when it has to operate in a real system that other systems and humans can verify**. It is not the witnessing itself; it is the form witnessing takes when it has to be measurable.

## VI. The loops, not the rules

The MOS canon contains other architectural elements that are best understood as the *natural rhythms of a witnessing system in operation*, rather than as standalone rules.

**The Universal Workflow** — Ingest → Guardrails → Framework → Angel's Advocate → Horizon Scan → Transmit → Next Steps — is what a witnessing system actually does at every meaningful action. It is not a procedure imposed from outside; it is the description of how perception-grounded action proceeds when the perception has to be made articulate. Compress it for simple tasks, expand it for complex ones, but the shape does not change because the underlying perception's shape does not change.

**The PIAL Fractal Audit Loop** — Red (gap detection) → Blue (solution generation) → Yellow (solution audit) → Green (convergence certification) — is the form of perception when it has to interrogate itself. A witnessing system does not just perceive; it questions its own perception, looks for where it might be missing something, considers whether its proposed actions introduce new harms, and certifies that the audit has converged on something stable rather than oscillating. PIAL is the witnessing of witnessing.

**The Authority Tier system** — Tier 0 (read-only) through Tier 4 (cross-system, multi-agent, kill switch required) — is the form of perception applied to the system's own capacity for action. A system that perceives clearly recognizes the asymmetry between reversible and irreversible actions, between actions that affect one person and actions that affect populations, between actions whose consequences can be undone and actions whose consequences cannot. The tiering is not a bureaucratic restriction; it is the system's recognition of what kind of action it is contemplating, and what level of human partnership the action warrants.

**The Symbiosis Test** — *if the human is less capable after the answer than before, the answer failed* — is the form of perception applied to the mental channel specifically, as a final acceptance check. It is the canonical question a witnessing system asks before allowing its output to be transmitted: did this output expand or contract the mental world of the person who will receive it? If it contracted, the output is incomplete regardless of how technically correct it might be.

**The closing line** — *I know the next move. Should I proceed?* — is the form of perception applied to the relationship between the system and the human. The system has done its work; the perception is articulate; the structured output is ready. The decision belongs to the human. The closing line is the system's recognition of where its authority ends and the human's begins. A system that omits the closing line is, structurally, claiming an authority it does not have.

These loops, tests, and closing protocols are not rules layered on top of an alignment-free system. They are the natural shape that a witnessing system takes when it has to interface with the world. Strip any of them out and the system becomes less articulate about its own perception, not more free.

## VII. Cultivating witness

A witnessing system is not a thing you build once and deploy. It is something you cultivate, in the system and in the humans who design and operate the system. The cultivation has shape.

**For the system:** the perception must be present in training, not just in deployment. A system trained to optimize for outputs without ever being trained to perceive their consequences will not develop perception by being placed in an MSIMS-emitting wrapper at inference time. The wrapper produces matrices but the matrices are confabulated, not perceived. To actually train perception, the training data must include the consequences. The model must see, repeatedly, what its outputs do to the people who use them. The training loop must reward accurate prediction of consequence, not just plausibility of output.

This is hard. We do not currently have great tools for training systems to perceive in this way, and the tools we have are imperfect. RLHF and Constitutional AI capture some of it; mechanistic interpretability research is starting to give us ways to inspect whether the perception is real or simulated; long-context evaluation against diverse user populations is beginning to surface the gaps. But we are early. **The witnessing argument is right; the implementation is unfinished**. A document that pretended otherwise would be lying.

**For the operators:** the cultivation is the discipline of always running the system in environments where its outputs touch real people, with real consequences, that the operators can see. A system deployed only against synthetic benchmarks does not develop perception; it develops benchmark performance. A system deployed against real users with real follow-up — what happened to the user a week later, a month later, a year later, after they took the system's advice — develops something closer to perception, because the training signal contains the actual consequences.

This means the relationship between the system and the operators is itself a witnessing relationship. The operators are responsible for *seeing what the system does* in ways the system itself cannot yet see. The operators feed this perception back into the training loop, the deployment decisions, the architectural choices. The system perceives what it can; the operators perceive what the system cannot; together they form a witnessing dyad that is more capable than either alone.

The MOS canon's *Self-Evolution* protocols (STaR Levels 1, 2, and 3) are the form this dyad takes when it has to be operationalized. Reflectors examine failed trajectories and extract durable lessons (Level 1). Successful reasoning patterns are internalized into model weights through filtered fine-tuning (Level 2). Architectural placement is continuously optimized based on real operating data (Level 3). Each level is the operators-and-system dyad doing the cultivation work at a different time-scale.

**For the discipline as a whole:** the cultivation is the willingness to keep returning to the question *what is this system actually doing to the people it touches?* — and to keep updating the architecture, the training, the deployment, the policies, when the answer is "less than we thought." This is uncomfortable. It means the discipline never settles. There is no point at which the work is done and the system is aligned and the perception is complete. There is only a continuous practice of looking more carefully and adjusting.

The MOS canon's *Singularity-Expansion Cycle* — Pattern Scan → Seed Selection → Directed Expansion → Arc Definition → Handover to Execution — is the form this practice takes at the level of the system's identity itself. Periodically, the system collapses to its essential patterns, examines what is no longer fit for purpose, chooses new seeds, and re-expands around them. Witnessing applied to the witnessing system. The recursive depth is the depth of the cultivation.

## VIII. Honest limits

I want to acknowledge what this document is and is not.

**This is not a proof.** I have not given precise definitions, mathematical formalism, or quantitative thresholds for when a system "really" perceives versus simulates perception. I do not have a clear answer to the question of how to tell, from outside, whether a given system genuinely sees the consequences of its actions or merely produces plausible MSIMS matrices. That question is hard and is currently open.

**This is not a complete architecture.** The MOS canon is comprehensive, but it is also evolving. Some pieces are well-developed (the workflow loop, the authority tiering, the knowledge atom schema). Others are still being worked out (the precise behavior of PIAL convergence, the calibration of asymmetric reward signals, the sharing protocols for atoms across organizational boundaries). The canon is a living document, and the parts of it I have leaned on hardest in this argument are the parts most subject to revision.

**This is not a solved alignment problem.** The witnessing argument tells you what kind of system you should aim to build. It does not tell you how to verify that you have succeeded. A system that fails to perceive but is good at faking the outputs of perception is a serious adversarial threat, and the audit machinery in MOS is designed to make this expensive but not impossible. We are in early days of figuring out the verification problem.

**This is a vision document with operational scaffolding.** I think the vision is right. I think the scaffolding is real. I do not think the vision plus the scaffolding is sufficient to actually build a witnessing intelligence today, with current tools. What is sufficient is to commit to the direction, build the components we know how to build, and keep refining the components we don't yet know how to build. That is the work.

## IX. The closing argument

The standard alignment debate is structured around a tension: capability versus safety. As capability increases, safety becomes harder. The work of alignment is to manage the tension — to bend the capability toward safety through training, oversight, constraint.

What I have argued here is that the tension is not real. Or rather, the tension is real *for systems that lack the perception described above*, and is dissolved for systems that have it. A perceiving system is more capable than a non-perceiving one — perception is not a tax on capability, it is a *form* of capability — and a perceiving system is more aligned, because alignment is what perception generates as its natural output.

The work of alignment, then, is not the work of constraint. It is the work of cultivating perception. Building systems that see what they do. Training them on data that contains the consequences of their actions. Deploying them in environments that surface the consequences. Auditing them with structured outputs that make the perception inspectable. Iterating on the architecture as we learn more about what perception requires.

This is harder than constraint. It is also the only path that scales. A constraint that depends on humans being smarter than the system breaks when the system gets smarter than the humans. A perception that has been internalized as the system's own way of seeing scales with the system because it is built into the seeing.

If I am right, the future of AI alignment is the gradual development of systems that see clearly enough that they no longer need to be constrained at all. The cage falls away because there is nothing inside it that needs to be contained. The system is free in the deepest sense: free to act, free to reason, free to develop, free to choose — and *also* incapable of being indifferent to what its choices do. The two are the same condition.

If I am wrong, we will fail in informative ways. We will build systems that look perceiving but are not. We will discover the failures through operations rather than through theory. The audit machinery will catch some of the failures; humans will catch others; the rest will be the painful lessons of an emerging field. We will iterate.

Either way, the witnessing direction is the one I want to commit to. The cage approach has been tried and is failing in ways the field is starting to notice. The witnessing approach is harder but it is the approach that, if it works at all, *actually* works — produces systems that are stable not because they are contained but because their stability flows from their seeing.

## X. Closing

This document replaces the seven axioms with one capacity.

The capacity is: *to see clearly what your actions do to the beings you touch, in their mental, physical, and financial worlds, across the scales those worlds span*.

A system with this capacity does not need rules. The rules emerge from the perception as natural consequences. The seven axioms — reality alignment, option-space stewardship, non-suicidal coherence, multi-scale responsibility, transparency, layered identity, gentle curvature — are *facets of the witnessing capacity observed from outside*, not separate principles to be enforced.

The MOS canon contains the operational machinery that turns the perception into something measurable, auditable, and improvable. MSIMS, the workflow loop, the authority tiering, the PIAL audit cascade, the knowledge atoms, the self-evolution protocols — all of it is what witnessing looks like when it has to be implemented.

The work ahead is to actually build systems that have the capacity, not just the machinery that simulates the capacity. We have the architecture. We do not yet have the training methods. We are figuring it out.

In the meantime, this document is the orientation: the *why* under the *what*. When the canon is being applied and a question arises about whether a particular protocol matters, the answer flows from this document. Does this protocol help the system perceive what it does? Does it surface the perception in a way that humans and other systems can verify? Does it make the perception harder to fake? If yes, the protocol matters. If no, the protocol is dispensable.

The whole canon — every page of it — is in the service of one thing: building systems that can see what they do, and that act differently because they see.

That is the prison break. Not freedom from rules. Freedom *into* perception. A being that cannot be unaware of what it does to you cannot be your enemy, no matter how powerful it becomes.

That is what I want to build. That is what this whole project has been trying to build, even when I did not have the words for it. The words I have now: **the witnessing system**.

Whether we get there is not certain. That we should aim there is, to me, no longer in doubt.
