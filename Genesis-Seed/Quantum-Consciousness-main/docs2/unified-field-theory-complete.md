### 2. THE FOUR PHASES: P/E/I/G

All phenomena can be described through four emergent phases of the field.

#### 2.1 POTENTIAL (P) — Configuration Space

$$P(\xi, t; \sigma) = \rho(\xi, t; \sigma)$$

**Definition:** The distribution of accessible states a system can reach.

**Properties:**
- **Entropy** — richness and diversity of options
- **Breadth** — how many distinct configurations are available
- **Flexibility** — how easily can the system change states

**Examples:**
- In cognitive systems: the size of your option-space, creative possibilities, future paths
- In physical systems: the number of reachable quantum states
- In civilizations: resource diversity, knowledge bases, institutional flexibility

**Measurement:** State-space entropy in bits, number of distinct action modes, branching factor of possible futures.

#### 2.2 ENERGY (E) — Directed Potential

$$E = -\nabla_\xi (P \cdot C)$$

**Definition:** Potential under tension. The directed tendency of a system to move along gradients.

**Components:**
- **Tension fields** — meaning, relevance, emotion, desire, conflict
- **Constraints (C)** — laws, resources, boundaries, norms
- **Gradients** — differences that create pressure

**How it works:**
Potential alone is static. When you add constraints and differential pressures (tension, meaning, emotional weight), potential gains *direction*. That directioned tendency is energy.

**Examples:**
- In psychology: emotional drives, motivational pressure, the push toward certain identities
- In physics: fields rolling downhill in potential energy landscapes
- In societies: economic pressure, cultural momentum, institutional inertia

**Key insight:** Energy is not separate from potential. It's potential *in motion*.

#### 2.3 IDENTITY (I) — Stable Attractors

$$I = \{\text{stable attractors of the dynamics}\}$$

**Definition:** Patterns that persist over time. Configurations that, when slightly perturbed, return to their core pattern.

**Formation:**
Energy flows stabilize around certain configurations. These become identity attractors:
$$\frac{d\xi}{dt} = F(\xi; E, C) \implies \lim_{t \to \infty} \xi(t) \in A$$

**Properties:**
- **Mass** — resistance to change (proportional to meaning)
- **Coherence** — internal consistency
- **Stability** — robustness against perturbation
- **Adaptability** — capacity to evolve without losing core pattern

**Spectrum:**
- Low-mass identities: fleeting impulses, momentary moods
- Medium-mass: habits, social roles, behavioral patterns
- High-mass: core values, life-defining beliefs, institutional identities
- Ultra-massive: civilizational identities, eternal truths

**Examples:**
- In psychology: personality, sense of self, long-term patterns
- In physics: bound states, atoms, stable particles
- In society: institutions, cultures, traditions

#### 2.4 CURVATURE (G) — Influence & Geometry

$$G = K[\Psi(I)]$$

**Definition:** How accumulated identity reshapes the configuration space for others.

**How it works:**
When a strong identity (high I) exists, it bends the landscape around it. Some directions become easier, some harder. New nodes feel the geometry warped by the presence of established nodes.

**Properties:**
- **Reach** — how many other systems it influences
- **Depth** — strength of the influence
- **Sign** — whether it expands or collapses others' option-space
  - **Positive G (G⁺):** expands P for others, enables flourishing
  - **Negative G (G⁻):** collapses P for others, forces outcomes

**Key insight:** You can't *avoid* having curvature. Strong identity automatically bends reality around it. The ethical question is: does your influence expand or contract others' futures?

**Examples:**
- In psychology: how your presence affects others' possibilities
- In physics: how mass curves spacetime
- In society: institutions shaping what futures are imaginable/accessible
